Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

Definitions, diagonalization, spectral theorem, spectral radius, Jordan form, Schur decomposition, SVD relations, Gershgorin, power method.

1) Definition & Characteristic Polynomial

An eigenpair $(\lambda,\mathbf{v}\neq \mathbf{0})$ of $A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ satisfies \[ A\mathbf{v}=\lambda \mathbf{v}. \] Eigenvalues are roots of the characteristic polynomial: \[ p_A(\lambda)=\det(\lambda I - A)=0. \]
For \( A=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix} \), \( p_A(\lambda)=\det\!\begin{bmatrix}\lambda-4&-1\\0&\lambda-2\end{bmatrix} =(\lambda-4)(\lambda-2). \) Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1=4$, $\lambda_2=2$.

2) Diagonalization

$A$ is diagonalizable if there exists an invertible $P$ s.t. \[ A = P D P^{-1},\qquad D=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n), \] where columns of $P$ are (linearly independent) eigenvectors. Sufficient condition: $n$ distinct eigenvalues.
For \( A=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix} \): \[ \lambda_1=4,\; \mathbf{v}_1=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix};\qquad \lambda_2=2,\; \mathbf{v}_2=\begin{bmatrix}-\tfrac12\\1\end{bmatrix}. \] Then \( P=\begin{bmatrix}1&-\tfrac12\\0&1\end{bmatrix},\; D=\begin{bmatrix}4&0\\0&2\end{bmatrix}, \) and $A=PDP^{-1}$.

3) Spectral Theorem (Real Symmetric)

If $A=A^\top$, then there exists an orthogonal $Q$ and real diagonal $\Lambda$ with \[ A=Q\Lambda Q^\top,\quad Q^\top Q=I. \] Eigenvectors can be chosen orthonormal; eigenvalues are real.
Let \( S=\begin{bmatrix}5&-2\\-2&5\end{bmatrix}. \) Eigenvalues: solve $\det(S-\lambda I)=(5-\lambda)^2-4=0$ ⇒ $\lambda\in\{3,7\}$. Orthonormal eigenvectors: \( \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(1,1)^\top \) for $\lambda=7$ and \( \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(1,-1)^\top \) for $\lambda=3$. Hence \[ S = Q\Lambda Q^\top,\quad Q=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix},\; \Lambda=\begin{bmatrix}7&0\\0&3\end{bmatrix}. \]

4) Spectral Radius & Gelfand’s Formula

The spectral radius is \[ \rho(A)=\max_i |\lambda_i(A)|. \] Gelfand’s formula (for any operator norm $\|\cdot\|$): \[ \rho(A)=\lim_{k\to\infty}\|A^k\|^{1/k}. \]
For $A=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix}$, eigenvalues are $4$ and $2$, so $\rho(A)=4$.

5) Jordan Normal Form

Over $\mathbb{C}$, every $A$ is similar to a Jordan matrix $J$: \[ A=PJP^{-1},\quad J=\operatorname{diag}(J_{k_1}(\lambda_1),\dots,J_{k_r}(\lambda_r)), \] where a Jordan block is \( J_k(\lambda)= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda&1&&\\&\lambda&\ddots&\\&&\ddots&1\\&&&\lambda \end{bmatrix}. \) $A$ is diagonalizable iff all Jordan blocks are size $1$.
\[ B=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix} \quad\text{has only one eigenvector}\quad \mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}. \] Its Jordan form is $J=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix}$ (already a single Jordan block).

6) Schur Decomposition

Over $\mathbb{C}$, any $A$ admits a unitary Schur decomposition \[ A = Q T Q^*,\quad Q^*Q=I, \] where $T$ is upper triangular with eigenvalues of $A$ on its diagonal.
Even when $A$ is not normal/diagonalizable, Schur form exists and is numerically stable; QR algorithm computes (real/complex) Schur form in practice.

7) SVD vs. Eigen-Decomposition

For $A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$, the SVD is \[ A=U\Sigma V^\top,\quad \Sigma=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_r>0). \] Singular values satisfy \[ \sigma_i^2 \ \text{are eigenvalues of } A^\top A \ (\text{and } AA^\top). \]
Take \( A=\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\0&1\end{bmatrix}. \) Then \( A^\top A=\begin{bmatrix}9&3\\3&2\end{bmatrix}. \) Eigenvalues of $A^\top A$ are roots of \( \lambda^2-11\lambda+9=0 \) ⇒ $\lambda_{1,2}=\frac{11\pm\sqrt{121-36}}{2}=\frac{11\pm\sqrt{85}}{2}$, so \( \sigma_{1,2}=\sqrt{\lambda_{1,2}}. \)

8) Gershgorin Circle Theorem

Every eigenvalue of $A=[a_{ij}]$ lies in at least one disk \[ D(a_{ii}, R_i),\quad R_i=\sum_{j\ne i}|a_{ij}|. \]
For \( A=\begin{bmatrix} 4 & -1 & 0\\ 2 & 3 & 1\\ 0 & -2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \), disks are \( D(4,1),\ D(3,|2|+|1|=3),\ D(2,2). \) All eigenvalues lie in the union of these disks in $\mathbb{C}$.

9) Rayleigh Quotient & Power Method

Rayleigh quotient for nonzero $x$: \[ R_A(x)=\frac{x^\top A x}{x^\top x}. \] For symmetric $A$, $R_A(x)\in[\lambda_{\min},\lambda_{\max}]$ and attains extrema at eigenvectors. Power method (dominant eigenpair): iterate $x_{k+1}=\frac{Ax_k}{\|Ax_k\|}$ to obtain the eigenvector of $\rho(A)$ (if unique in magnitude).
For \( S=\begin{bmatrix}5&-2\\-2&5\end{bmatrix} \), starting $x_0=(1,0)^\top$: \( x_1\propto Sx_0=(5,-2)^\top,\; x_2\propto Sx_1,\ \dots \) converges to eigenvector of $\lambda_{\max}=7$, i.e., $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(1,1)^\top$.

10) Matrix Functions via Spectral/Jordan Forms

If $A=PDP^{-1}$, \[ f(A)=P\,f(D)\,P^{-1},\quad f(D)=\operatorname{diag}(f(\lambda_i)). \] If $A=PJP^{-1}$ with Jordan $J$, then $f(J)$ uses block-wise series: \[ f(J_k(\lambda))= \begin{bmatrix} f(\lambda) & f'(\lambda) & \cdots \\ & f(\lambda) & \ddots \\ && \ddots \end{bmatrix}. \]
For $A$ diagonalizable, $e^{tA}=P\,e^{tD}\,P^{-1}$ with $e^{tD}=\operatorname{diag}(e^{t\lambda_i})$. For the defective block $J_2(\lambda)$, \( e^{tJ_2(\lambda)}=e^{t\lambda}\begin{bmatrix}1&t\\0&1\end{bmatrix}. \)

Summary Properties

  • Diagonalizable $\iff$ $n$ independent eigenvectors (over $\mathbb{C}$).
  • Spectral theorem: symmetric $A$ diagonalized by orthogonal $Q$.
  • Schur: $A=QTQ^*$ with $T$ upper triangular (eigs on diagonal).
  • $\rho(A)=\max|\lambda_i|$, equals $\lim_{k\to\infty}\|A^k\|^{1/k}$.
  • Gershgorin bounds localize eigenvalues.
  • SVD always exists (rectangular too); $\sigma_i^2$ are eigenvalues of $A^\top A$.
  • Power method converges to dominant eigenvector under mild gaps.
